# OR Suite Renovation Planning Guide for Texas Hospitals

Operating room renovations are among the most complex, high-stakes projects a hospital facilities team can undertake. Unlike administrative offices or waiting rooms, the OR cannot tolerate extended downtime — every day a suite is out of service represents lost revenue, deferred procedures, and pressure on the remaining rooms. A well-planned OR renovation minimizes disruption while delivering a surgical environment that meets current regulatory standards, supports your clinical team, and extends usable life by 15–25 years.

This guide is written for Texas hospital facility directors, plant operations managers, and OR managers who are planning or evaluating an OR suite renovation. It draws on the experience of Surgiquip Solutions — authorized Skytron dealer for Texas since 1983 — and the technical realities we've encountered in installations across Houston, San Antonio, Lubbock, El Paso, and beyond.

## Phase 1: Scope Assessment and Needs Analysis

### Define the Renovation Driver

OR renovations are typically triggered by one or more of the following:

| Driver | Typical Scope |
|--------|---------------|
| Equipment end-of-life | OR table, lights, and/or boom replacement; partial renovation |
| Regulatory citation | TJC, DNV GL, or CMS deficiency requiring infrastructure correction |
| Program expansion | Adding hybrid OR capability, robotic surgery suite, or additional OR volume |
| HVAC/mechanical failure | Forced renovation of air handling, HEPA filtration, or pressure controls |
| Fire/life safety upgrade | Egress, sprinkler, or electrical panel replacement triggering full renovation |
| Strategic investment | Competitive differentiation — new service lines, physician recruitment |

The driver determines scope. A single-room equipment swap (OR table + lights) is fundamentally different from a hybrid OR conversion that requires structural reinforcement, additional MEP capacity, and radiation shielding.

### Conduct a Baseline Assessment

Before engaging architects or contractors, collect baseline data on every affected suite:

**Equipment inventory:**
- Current OR table model, year, load capacity, and maintenance history
- Surgical light make/model, installation date, lux output (test at 90 cm below table surface)
- Ceiling boom make/model, payload, arm reach, utility connections (medical gas, electrical, data)
- Imaging equipment (C-arm, O-arm, ceiling-mounted flat panel for hybrid ORs)
- Anesthesia equipment, boom utility connections

**Structural:**
- Ceiling height (floor to structural deck) — 11 ft minimum for standard OR, 13+ ft for hybrid OR
- Load-bearing capacity at ceiling suspension points (minimum 500 lbs per point for ceiling booms)
- Existing beam/deck configuration and clearance for new mounting hardware

**MEP (Mechanical, Electrical, Plumbing):**
- Medical gas: O₂, N₂O, medical air, vacuum, CO₂ — flow rates, pressure, manifold locations
- Electrical: panel capacity, circuit counts, isolated power panel (IPP) compliance with NFPA 99
- HVAC: air exchanges per hour (minimum 15 ACH per FGI Guidelines), HEPA filtration condition, pressure differential (positive pressure vs. scrub corridors)
- Data/IT: OR integration system, OR lights with integrated cameras, video routing infrastructure

## Phase 2: Regulatory and Standards Framework

Texas hospital OR renovations are governed by multiple overlapping regulatory frameworks. Understand all applicable standards before schematic design begins.

### Facility Guidelines Institute (FGI) Guidelines

The FGI *Guidelines for Design and Construction of Hospitals* is the primary reference for OR design in Texas. Key requirements include:

- **Minimum OR size:** 400 sq ft (standard OR); 600 sq ft (hybrid OR)
- **Clear floor space:** 48 inches from operating table edge to fixed equipment/cabinets on three sides
- **HVAC:** Minimum 15 air changes per hour (ACH) total; minimum 3 ACH fresh air; laminar airflow over the sterile field recommended for orthopedic/implant procedures
- **Temperature range:** 68°F–75°F, humidity 20%–60%

### NFPA 99: Health Care Facilities Code

Critical for Texas OR electrical infrastructure:

- **Category 1 spaces** (OR): Isolated power panel (IPP) required, line isolation monitor (LIM) mandatory
- **Medical gas:** NFPA 99 Chapter 5 — zone valve placement, alarm panels, piping color coding, outlet labeling
- **Emergency power:** Automatic transfer to emergency branch within 10 seconds

### TJC / CMS Requirements

Any renovation that changes the OR footprint, ventilation, or electrical infrastructure requires:

- New or updated Environment of Care (EC) risk assessments
- Updated Infection Control Risk Assessment (ICRA) during construction
- Documentation that all systems meet current standards (FGI, NFPA 99, ASHRAE 170)

### Texas DSHS Healthcare Facility Regulation

Texas DSHS requires submission and review for major construction or renovation at licensed healthcare facilities. Coordinate with your Texas DSHS regional office early to understand submission requirements, review timelines, and inspection scheduling.

## Phase 3: Equipment Specification

### OR Table Selection

The OR table is the centerpiece of the surgical suite. Key specifications to evaluate:

| Specification | Standard General Surgery | Orthopedic/Trauma | Hybrid OR |
|---------------|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Table weight capacity | 450–500 lbs | 800+ lbs | 500+ lbs |
| Radiolucent top | Required for C-arm use | Essential | Fully radiolucent |
| Table drive | Electric, motorized | Hydraulic/electric | Motorized with carbon fiber top |
| Extension capability | Head/leg extension | Full orthopedic attachments | Modular — cardiac, neuro, vascular |
| Height range | 28"–44" | 20"–42" | 28"–44" |

Skytron tables are designed for direct integration with Skytron lights and ceiling booms — reduced installation complexity, single-vendor service accountability.

### Surgical Lighting

**Key metrics for surgical light specification:**

- **Illuminance at the surgical field:** Minimum 40,000 lux; high-performance suites target 120,000–160,000 lux
- **Illuminance uniformity (E1/E8 ratio):** ≥0.75 for IEC 60601-2-41 compliance
- **Color Rendering Index (CRI):** ≥95 for accurate tissue differentiation
- **Color temperature:** 4,000–4,500K for standard OR; surgeon preference may vary
- **Heat at field (infrared at 1m):** ≤1,000 W/m² to protect sterile draping and sensitive tissue
- **Shadow management:** Adjustable focus, satellite heads, optional arm-mounted secondary heads

### Ceiling Boom Systems

OR ceiling booms mount to the structural deck and carry medical gases, electrical circuits, data/video connections, and equipment arms to the point of use — eliminating floor clutter, trip hazards, and tangled cords.

**For the anesthesia team:**
- Medical gas outlets: O₂, N₂O, medical air, vacuum, CO₂
- Electrical: 4–8 outlets (isolated power), 120V and 220V options
- Data: Ethernet, video output, patient monitoring integration
- Equipment shelf: Anesthesia machine, infusion pump bars, gas analyzer

**For the surgical team (equipment boom):**
- Single-arm vs. dual-arm vs. bridge configurations (see ceiling boom systems guide)
- Payload capacity: minimum 330 lbs per arm for video system + monitor integration
- Arm reach: 47–63 inches depending on OR table positioning requirements

## Phase 4: Phased Construction Planning

### The OR Downtime Problem

The single most important factor in OR renovation planning is minimizing downtime. Texas hospital OR suites typically generate $2,500–$6,000 per room-hour in direct revenue — a 4-week room closure at 10 procedures/day represents $700,000–$1.7M in deferred revenue plus indirect costs.

**Strategies for minimizing downtime:**

**Option 1: Modular swap**
Schedule the entire room for a concentrated shutdown (10–20 working days). Pre-fabricate all equipment mounting assemblies, ceiling boom hardware, and electrical rough-in at an off-site staging area. Install in sequence: structural, MEP rough-in, mechanical, equipment. This requires surgical volume redistribution but minimizes the construction calendar.

**Option 2: Phased shell-and-fit-out**
Complete MEP rough-in and structural work during scheduled PM downtime (nights/weekends). Fit-out equipment installation in a final 5–7 day window. Reduces single-block downtime but extends the construction calendar by 8–12 weeks.

**Option 3: Swing room utilization**
If your facility has an underutilized suite, designate it as the swing room for displaced procedures. Requires OR scheduling flexibility and may require interim equipment rental (OR table, lights).

### Construction Sequencing for a Standard OR Renovation

For a single OR suite renovation with equipment replacement:

| Week | Activity |
|------|----------|
| W1–W2 | Structural work: ceiling deck prep, mounting plate installation, blocking |
| W3–W4 | MEP rough-in: medical gas piping, electrical conduit, HVAC duct modifications |
| W5–W6 | MEP finish: medical gas outlets, IPP installation, HEPA unit installation |
| W7 | OR equipment installation: ceiling boom mounting, OR table delivery, surgical light installation |
| W8 | Systems integration: boom utility connections, OR table electrical, light arm commissioning |
| W9 | Testing and commissioning: pressure testing, LIM testing, light output verification, HVAC balance |
| W10 | Punch list, staff training, regulatory inspection preparation |

## Phase 5: Infection Control During Renovation

OR renovations in operating hospitals require a rigorous Infection Control Risk Assessment (ICRA) to prevent airborne contamination of surgical suites during construction.

**ICRA Class IV protocols (required for OR renovation):**

- Negative pressure barrier rooms isolating the construction zone from the surgical corridor
- HEPA-filtered air supply with continuous monitoring
- Sticky mat entry control at all construction zone entry points
- Daily disposal of construction debris in sealed containers through dedicated waste routes (not through patient care areas)
- Pre-activity and post-activity cultures per infection control protocol
- Project-specific infection control sign-off before OR re-commissioning

## Phase 6: Commissioning and Regulatory Sign-Off

A completed OR renovation is not complete until every system is tested, documented, and accepted by the appropriate parties.

### Commissioning Checklist

**Medical gas systems:**
- Pressure tests per NFPA 99 Chapter 5 (200% of working pressure for 24 hours)
- Cross-connection tests at every outlet (use outlet testers)
- Flow and pressure testing at each zone
- Alarm panel functional test

**Electrical systems:**
- LIM functional test and documentation
- IPP resistance test
- Emergency power transfer test (automatic and manual)
- Equipment ground fault testing

**HVAC systems:**
- Air balance report (ACH verification at each supply and exhaust diffuser)
- HEPA filter integrity test (DOP/PAO challenge)
- Pressure differential verification vs. adjacent corridors
- Temperature and humidity monitoring over 24-hour period

**OR equipment:**
- OR table load test (rated capacity, all tilt/height functions)
- Surgical light lux output measurement (at 90 cm below table, multiple field positions)
- Ceiling boom payload test and rotation sweep
- Integrated video/OR integration system functional test

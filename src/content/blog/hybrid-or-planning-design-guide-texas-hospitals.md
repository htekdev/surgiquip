A hybrid operating room is one of the most complex — and most valuable — capital investments a hospital system can make. Combining the capabilities of a traditional surgical suite with advanced interventional imaging, a well-designed hybrid OR allows vascular surgeons, cardiologists, and cardiothoracic teams to perform minimally invasive procedures that previously required two separate rooms, two separate teams, and sometimes two separate transfers.

Surgiquip Solutions has been involved in hybrid OR installations across Southeast Texas for over a decade — including hybrid rooms at major Houston-area hospital systems featuring Siemens and Philips imaging platforms integrated with Skytron ceiling-mount boom systems, Aurora LED surgical lights, and 6700B/6701 surgical tables. This guide covers the planning framework that makes hybrid OR projects succeed.

## What Is a Hybrid OR?

A hybrid OR is a surgical suite equipped with fixed interventional imaging — typically a ceiling-mounted C-arm or flat-panel detector system from Siemens (Zeego, Artis), Philips (Allura), or GE — integrated into a full operating room environment with:

- Ceiling-mounted equipment and anesthesia booms
- Full surgical lighting (typically LED with shadow control)
- A surgical table capable of carbon-fiber top configurations for intraoperative imaging
- Complete sterile environment (HEPA/laminar airflow, positive pressure)
- Full surgical team support: scrub/circulator positions, anesthesia bay, instrument tables

The result: endovascular, transcatheter, and hybrid surgical procedures — TAVR, EVAR, TEVAR, hybrid coronary revascularization, neurovascular interventions — can be performed with the imaging tools of a cath lab and the surgical safety net of a full OR.

## Clinical Use Cases That Drive Hybrid OR Demand in Texas

Texas's large hospital systems have high procedural volumes in cardiovascular and vascular surgery — exactly the specialties that benefit most from hybrid OR capability. Common procedures performed in hybrid ORs include:

**Vascular Surgery**
- EVAR (endovascular aortic repair) and complex EVAR with fenestrated/branched stent grafts
- TEVAR (thoracic endovascular aortic repair)
- Carotid stenting and hybrid carotid endarterectomy
- Lower extremity revascularization combining open and endovascular approaches

**Cardiac Surgery**
- TAVR (transcatheter aortic valve replacement)
- Hybrid coronary revascularization (HCR)
- Lead extraction with fluoroscopic guidance
- Mitral valve procedures

**Neurovascular**
- Cerebral aneurysm clipping with intraoperative angiography
- AVM resection with real-time imaging

When you examine the project history of a facility like **Houston Methodist Sugar Land Hospital** — which required 2 hybrid ORs, 4 cardiovascular ORs, and a Cath Lab in a single installation — the complexity of the planning requirement becomes clear. Equipment selection, structural engineering, imaging positioning, and boom placement all had to be coordinated before a single anchor was installed.

## Phase 1: Imaging System Selection and Room Sizing

The imaging system is the single biggest driver of hybrid OR design. Every other element — boom placement, table position, room size, ceiling grid, floor loading — flows from the imaging vendor selection.

### Major Imaging Platforms

| Platform | Vendor | Typical Configuration |
|----------|--------|-----------------------|
| Artis Zeego | Siemens Healthineers | Multi-axis robotic C-arm on floor-mounted rail or ceiling mount |
| Artis icono/pheno | Siemens Healthineers | Biplane and single-plane configurations |
| Allura Xper | Philips | Single or biplane, ceiling or floor mounted |
| Discovery IGS | GE Healthcare | Ceiling or floor mount, various detector sizes |

Each platform has different spatial envelopes, weight requirements, and isocenter positioning needs. Room sizing minimums range from approximately 800–1,100 sq ft (net floor area) depending on the platform and whether it's single-plane or biplane. Your imaging vendor will provide a detailed room design guide — but your surgical equipment supplier needs to be at the table early because boom placement and surgical table positioning must be coordinated with the imaging isocenter.

### Structural Requirements

**Floor loading:** Fixed imaging systems — especially robotic platforms like the Siemens Zeego — impose significant point loads. Structural engineers must calculate the concentrated load at the rail or floor-mount pedestal location. Slab reinforcement is frequently required in new construction; existing floor slabs in renovation projects almost always require engineering evaluation.

**Ceiling grid:** Ceiling-mount imaging systems (many Philips Allura and GE IGS configurations) transfer load to the ceiling structure. Boom systems for anesthesia and equipment also load the ceiling. In a hybrid OR, the ceiling may simultaneously carry:
- 1–2 imaging system rails (1,000–4,000 lbs each depending on platform)
- 2–4 equipment/anesthesia booms (400–800 lbs each with equipment loaded)
- 2 surgical light heads (100–300 lbs per head)
- AV/integration pendants

Structural engineers must design the ceiling grid for all of these loads acting simultaneously. This coordination between the structural team, imaging vendor, and surgical equipment supplier is one of the most time-sensitive steps in a hybrid OR project.

## Phase 2: Boom System Design

Equipment ceiling booms in a hybrid OR serve multiple roles: they deliver gas (O₂, N₂O, medical air, vacuum, data/power) to the surgical and anesthesia positions, organize cables and equipment, and must do all of this while leaving the imaging system's sweep path completely clear.

### The Sweep Path Problem

The most common coordination failure in hybrid OR planning is **boom placement that blocks the imaging system's sweep path**. A ceiling-mounted flat panel detector needs a clear arc of travel — sometimes exceeding 270 degrees — to reposition between anterior-posterior (AP) and lateral views, and to park out of the sterile field during open portions of a hybrid procedure.

Before finalizing boom positions, your surgical equipment supplier should obtain the imaging vendor's **sweep path diagram** and model boom placement around it. Skytron's ceiling-mount boom systems are configurable with extended horizontal reach to push anesthesia booms and equipment booms outside the imaging travel zone.

### Boom Configuration in a Hybrid OR

A typical hybrid OR boom layout includes:

**Anesthesia Zone (head of table)**
- Anesthesia boom with gas outlets (O₂, N₂O, medical air, vacuum, AGSS), electrical, data
- Positioned to clear imaging travel arc — often extended laterally or toward room corner

**Equipment Zone (sides)**
- One or two equipment booms for monitor arms, power, data
- Must accommodate large flat-panel displays used during endovascular procedures
- Some configurations use dedicated monitor-arm booms separate from gas-service booms

**Patient Zone (around table)**
- Minimal fixed overhead equipment — imaging system needs unobstructed access
- Scrub/circulator service typically from wall panels or repositionable booms

Surgiquip has configured Skytron boom systems in hybrid environments ranging from single-arm configurations in smaller hybrid rooms (like the **Memorial Hermann Heart & Vascular Institute** hybrid OR) to extended-arm, multi-boom configurations in high-volume cardiovascular suites.

## Phase 3: Surgical Lighting for Hybrid ORs

Standard surgical lighting in a hybrid OR requires more careful placement than in a conventional room. The lighting system must:

- Provide adequate illuminance (≥160,000 lux at primary field) during open portions of procedures
- Park completely clear of the imaging sweep path
- Not create X-ray shadow artifacts when the imaging system is in use
- Support in-light camera capability for documentation

### LED Light Head Selection

**Skytron Aurora LED** systems are well-suited for hybrid OR environments because of their compact head profile (reducing ceiling real estate consumed) and flexible arm configurations. The single-head Aurora has been specified in multiple Surgiquip hybrid OR installations across Houston because its slim profile minimizes interference with imaging positioning.

For hybrid ORs where biplane imaging is installed, some facilities specify a **single overhead light head** rather than dual heads to minimize the ceiling footprint and maximize imaging access. This is a clinical trade-off that perioperative leadership and the proceduralist team should evaluate together.

## Phase 4: Surgical Table Selection

The surgical table in a hybrid OR must serve two distinct functions:
1. Standard surgical positioning for the open or laparoscopic portion of a hybrid procedure
2. Intraoperative imaging compatibility — specifically, a **carbon fiber top** that is radiolucent (allows X-rays to pass without artifact)

### Carbon Fiber Top Requirements

Not all surgical tables ship with carbon fiber tops as standard equipment. For hybrid OR applications:
- Confirm carbon fiber top availability for the specific table model
- Verify radiolucency across the imaging area of interest (typically full-length carbon fiber inserts)
- Confirm the table's compatibility with the imaging vendor's specified table clearance dimensions — imaging isocenter height must align with table height at relevant patient positions

**Skytron 6700B** and **6701 surgical tables** used across multiple Surgiquip installations support carbon fiber top configurations for intraoperative imaging. Your equipment supplier should coordinate table specifications directly with the imaging vendor during the design phase.

## Phase 5: AV/Integration and Documentation Systems

Hybrid ORs generate significant volumes of imaging data — angiographic runs, fluoroscopy, 3D reconstructions — that must integrate with the hospital's PACS and clinical documentation systems. The AV integration layer includes:

- **Video routing:** Bringing interventional imaging video to OR displays and vice versa
- **DICOM integration:** Routing imaging data to PACS and archiving systems
- **SkyVision (Skytron):** Where deployed in Surgiquip installations, Skytron's SkyVision integration platform handles in-room AV, camera control, and documentation workflows

**Clear Lake Regional Medical Center** (Webster, TX) — a 10-OR facility that included 3 C-Section suites and 6 LDRP rooms in the Surgiquip scope — used **SkyVision integration** throughout, demonstrating how imaging and documentation integration scales from individual hybrid rooms to full surgical suites.

Early engagement of the hospital's IT/biomed and PACS teams is essential. Integration work frequently lies on the critical path for project completion.

## Phase 6: HVAC, Laminar Airflow, and NFPA 99 Compliance

Hybrid ORs are classified as **invasive procedure rooms** under NFPA 99 Healthcare Facilities Code and must meet:

- **Positive pressure** relative to adjacent spaces
- **Minimum 20 air changes per hour** (ACH), with at least 4 ACH outside air
- **HEPA filtration** in the supply air stream
- **Laminar or unidirectional airflow** in the zone above the sterile field

The imaging system's mechanical components (X-ray tube, detector electronics) generate heat that must be accounted for in the room's cooling load calculations. Additionally, if a ceiling-mount imaging system is installed, the laminar airflow diffuser array must be designed around the imaging rail — which often requires close coordination between the mechanical engineer and the imaging vendor.

Gas outlets (O₂, N₂O, medical air, vacuum, AGSS) for the boom system must be specified per NFPA 99 and the relevant zone classification. Boom-delivered gas requires zone valve coordination and typically a zone valve box located outside the imaging sweep path.

## Lessons from Southeast Texas Hybrid OR Projects

Based on Surgiquip's project history across the Houston metro area, several patterns repeat in hybrid OR installations:

**1. Start structural engineering early.** Structural engineering is consistently on the critical path. The longer imaging vendor selection is delayed, the later structural design begins — compressing the schedule toward construction start.

**2. Boom placement requires the imaging sweep path diagram.** Never finalize boom positions without the vendor's sweep path drawing in hand. Ceiling-mount boom positions locked in before this data arrives have been redesigned on every project where this sequencing was reversed.

**3. Table-imaging clearance must be verified, not assumed.** Carbon fiber table tops vary in radiolucent area. Verify the specific imaging area covers your proceduralist's clinical requirements before equipment is ordered.

**4. AV/PACS integration is rarely "simple."** Budget time (and scope) for IT/biomed coordination. Integration timelines almost always exceed initial estimates.

**5. Plan for the hybrid schedule shift.** A hybrid OR that serves cardiovascular surgery during the day may serve general surgery evenings and weekends. Storage, equipment positioning, and room reset protocols need to be designed in from the beginning.

## Getting Started: Questions for Your Surgical Equipment Supplier

Before engaging a surgical equipment supplier for a hybrid OR project, clarify:

- Can they provide **documented hybrid OR installation history** in your region? (Not generic OR experience — hybrid-specific experience.)
- Are their technicians **certified on the boom systems** they're installing? Factory training for ceiling-mount boom systems is not universal.
- Can they coordinate **directly with your imaging vendor** on sweep path and table clearance?
- Do they have a **dedicated project manager** for hybrid OR projects of this scale?

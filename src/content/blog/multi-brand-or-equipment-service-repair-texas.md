## Why Single-Brand OR Fleets Are the Exception

Hospital purchasing teams are sometimes advised to standardize around a single manufacturer — tables, lights, booms, sterilizers — to simplify training and reduce the number of service relationships. This works well on paper and occasionally in practice. In the real world, hospitals accumulate equipment across budget cycles, clinical champions advocate for specific products they used during fellowship, group purchasing agreements shift every three years, and renovation projects are often funded in phases.

The result: most Texas hospital OR departments are managing a mix of Skytron tables alongside Steris or Stryker units acquired a decade earlier. Skytron Aurora LED lights installed during a recent renovation paired with older Berchtold pendants that still work fine and are not worth replacing. A Knight instrument washer next to a Tuttnauer autoclave purchased directly from the manufacturer's distributor.

When any of those units needs repair, the facilities manager needs someone who can respond — and who is not going to recommend replacement simply because the unit in question is outside their catalog.

## What "Multi-Brand Service" Actually Means

The phrase "multi-brand service capability" appears on a lot of vendor websites. It is worth understanding what separates genuine capability from a marketing claim.

**Factory-trained certification** means the technician has completed the manufacturer's own training program, has access to manufacturer technical documentation, and can order genuine OEM replacement parts. This is the highest tier of service qualification. Surgiquip technicians carry factory certifications from Skytron, Midmark, and Tuttnauer — the three manufacturers where certification is most clinically consequential given their equipment's role in sterile processing and critical care environments.

**ISO third-party service** is the industry term for qualified service on a manufacturer's equipment provided by someone other than the OEM authorized service network. For surgical tables, overhead lights, and ceiling booms that are past OEM service contract age, qualified ISO service is often the most practical and cost-effective option. The key qualifiers are documented training records, access to parts, and demonstrated repair history on that equipment type.

**Working knowledge** covers equipment that a service organization handles regularly enough to diagnose and repair reliably — not through formal certification, but through accumulated hands-on experience. Honest service partners are transparent about which category each piece of equipment falls into.

## The Facilities Manager's Service Decision Framework

When a piece of OR equipment needs attention, the facilities team is working through a fast-moving decision tree — often with a procedure suite that cannot afford an extended downtime. Here is a framework that clarifies the key decision points:

### Is This an Emergency or a Scheduled Repair?

Emergency repairs — a table that fails to lock position mid-procedure, a sterilizer that generates a fault code during a high-volume OR day, a boom arm that will not retract — require a partner with geographic proximity and dispatch capability. At Surgiquip's response window, most Houston-area facilities can have a technician on-site the same day for urgent situations. UTMB John Sealy in Galveston, North Cypress Medical Center in Cypress, and Memorial Hermann Northeast in Humble are all within the service corridor where rapid response is realistic.

Non-emergency repairs allow time to compare service options, obtain quotes, and schedule around the OR's case volume. This is where multi-year preventive maintenance agreements provide the most value — because the same partner managing your scheduled PMs already has baseline documentation on your equipment and is the natural first call when unplanned repairs arise.

### OEM Contract vs. Independent Service Agreement?

For equipment still under manufacturer warranty or within the first service cycle, OEM contract service is usually the right choice — the manufacturer carries responsibility for parts reliability and documentation accuracy. Once equipment moves past the warranty period and initial service contract, independent service agreements from a qualified ISO provider often deliver equivalent quality at lower cost.

The key factors in this decision:

- **Parts availability:** OEM service contracts typically guarantee OEM parts. Independent service providers should commit in writing to using genuine OEM parts or approved equivalent parts, with documentation of parts sourcing.
- **Response time SLAs:** OEM national service centers often have longer dispatch windows than regional independent providers. A Houston-based provider can commit to next-day response on most equipment classes; a national OEM dispatch center may not.
- **Calibration documentation:** Surgical tables and sterilizers require documented calibration records for Joint Commission surveys. Confirm that any service provider — OEM or independent — delivers these records in a format your biomedical team can file.
- **Cost:** Multi-year independent service agreements on a mature equipment fleet typically run 20–35% lower than OEM contracts on equivalent coverage. For a large OR department, that margin is meaningful.

### Service vs. Replace?

This is the decision where facilities managers most benefit from an honest service partner — one who is not incentivized to recommend replacement simply because it generates a larger transaction.

The practical decision framework:

**Replace if:**
- Repair cost exceeds 60% of current replacement value
- OEM has end-of-life'd the equipment (parts are unavailable and will remain unavailable)
- Clinical requirements have changed in ways the equipment cannot meet (e.g., a general surgery table in a department converting to bariatric subspecialty volume)
- Downtime frequency from that unit is disrupting OR throughput more than once per quarter

**Repair if:**
- The failure mode is a known wear item (hydraulic seals, actuator motors, positioning cylinders, locking mechanisms)
- Parts are available, the equipment is otherwise in good condition, and years of service life remain
- The unit has been recently calibrated and its performance record is otherwise clean
- The procedure mix in that room does not require capabilities the current equipment lacks

## Surgical Table Service: The High-Stakes Category

Of all the equipment in an OR suite, the surgical table generates the most service calls and carries the highest consequence for improper maintenance. A table that fails to maintain position during a procedure, loses hydraulic pressure, or has a compromised locking mechanism is a patient safety issue — not a convenience problem.

**Common surgical table failure modes and their causes:**

**Hydraulic drift:** The table slowly changes position after being locked. Usually caused by worn hydraulic seals, contaminated hydraulic fluid, or a faulty solenoid valve. Requires hydraulic system inspection, fluid flush, and seal replacement. Most tables remain repairable for this failure mode through 15+ years of service life.

**Actuator motor failures:** Electric surgical tables use linear actuators to drive Trendelenburg, reverse Trendelenburg, lateral tilt, and height adjustment. Actuator motors have defined service life cycles and can be replaced on most Skytron, Steris, Stryker, and Berchtold models without replacing the entire table.

**Positioning lock failure:** The mechanisms that lock each axis of movement experience wear proportional to OR volume. High-volume trauma centers and orthopedic surgery departments see this category of failure earlier in a table's service life. Documented PMs that include lock system testing and lubrication significantly extend the interval between lock repairs.

**Carbon fiber top damage:** Skytron's higher-end table series use carbon fiber tops to allow intraoperative imaging. Carbon fiber panels are repairable in many cases and replaceable in all cases — and given the imaging workflow benefits they provide, repair or panel replacement is almost always preferable to switching table platforms.

## Ceiling Boom System Service

Ceiling-mount boom systems — the articulating arms that deliver medical gases, electrical power, data connectivity, and in many configurations, equipment mounting for anesthesia machines and documentation systems — are among the most expensive and most overlooked items in an OR service program.

The oversight is understandable. When a boom system is working, it is invisible. When it fails during a procedure — a gas port that will not seal, a joint that will not lock, a monitor arm that drifts — it becomes immediately visible.

**Critical boom service points:**

**Gas port and seal integrity:** NFPA 99 Healthcare Facilities Code requires gas system testing and documentation. Boom service should include pressure testing of all gas ports and documentation of results. This is not optional; it is a compliance requirement.

**Joint lock testing:** Each articulating joint in a boom system has a locking mechanism that prevents unintended movement under load. These locks experience wear proportional to OR volume. Joint lock testing should be part of every annual PM.

**Cable management and electrical integrity:** Boom systems route significant electrical loads and sensitive data cables through articulating joints. Visual inspection and continuity testing of cable routing is a standard PM component that catches developing failures before they cause service interruption.

**SkyVision integration touchpoints:** In OR suites where Skytron SkyVision integration connects boom-mounted monitors with the table's positioning data, the integration electronics require their own service documentation. Surgiquip's technicians are trained on SkyVision integration across the Houston-area installations where this system is deployed, including Clear Lake Regional Medical Center (Webster), Memorial Hermann Sugar Land, and the North Cypress Medical Center in Cypress.

## Sterilization Equipment: The Compliance-Critical Category

Sterile processing equipment occupies a unique category in the service decision framework: it is not just a performance issue, it is a compliance issue. A sterilizer with inadequate documentation — missing biological indicator records, undocumented service intervals, absent calibration logs — is a Joint Commission deficiency finding waiting to happen.

Tuttnauer autoclaves are among the most widely deployed sterilizers in Texas hospitals and ambulatory surgery centers. Surgiquip's factory certification from Tuttnauer means that service documentation carries the manufacturer's backing — an important distinction during accreditation surveys.

Key sterilization equipment service requirements:

**Preventive maintenance frequency:** Gravity sterilizers require PM at minimum annually; prevacuum units, monthly cycle qualification plus annual PM. Washer-disinfectors require quarterly PM at minimum.

**Documentation standards:** Every service event must be documented with date, technician credentials, work performed, parts replaced, and test results. AAMI ST79 is the applicable standard for documentation requirements.

**Biological indicator response:** When a sterilizer fails a biological indicator test, the response protocol requires immediate load recall, sterilizer quarantine, technical inspection, and corrective action documentation before return to service. Having a service partner on call with same-day response capability is the difference between a controlled event and a clinical operations disruption.

## Building a Service Partnership That Covers the Whole Fleet

The most efficient model for a multi-unit, multi-brand OR fleet is a primary service relationship with a single partner who can manage the breadth of your equipment — complemented by OEM contracts on specific units where manufacturer-only service is required by warranty terms.

What this looks like in practice at Surgiquip's client facilities:

1. **Fleet inventory audit** — document every piece of OR equipment, current service contract status, remaining warranty coverage, and last service date. This is often more labor-intensive than expected; Surgiquip's team can assist with this baseline documentation as part of a service agreement onboarding.

2. **PM schedule development** — map equipment service requirements to a calendar that aligns with OR scheduling patterns. High-volume ORs require service windows that match low-census periods. The PM schedule should be reflected in the CMMS (Computerized Maintenance Management System) used by the biomedical team.

3. **Emergency response protocol** — define the escalation path for equipment failures during active OR schedule. Know the service provider's dispatch number, expected response time for your location, and the backup equipment options for each critical item.

4. **Parts procurement clarity** — confirm in writing whether your service agreement covers parts or bills them separately, and whether OEM or equivalent parts are used. This affects both cost planning and compliance documentation.

5. **Annual review** — an effective service partnership includes an annual fleet review where repair history, failure patterns, and replacement candidates are evaluated together. Units with escalating repair frequency are better identified during a planned review than discovered when they fail during a case.

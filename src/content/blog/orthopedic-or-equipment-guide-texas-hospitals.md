The surgical table must accommodate extreme patient positioning — steep Trendelenburg, reverse Trendelenburg, lateral decubitus, prone, and seated positions. The boom system must provide utility access at positions dictated by orthopedic C-arm imaging workflows. The surgical lighting must deliver high-intensity, shadow-free illumination into deep surgical fields without obstructing the C-arm arc.

## What Makes an Orthopedic OR Different

The design of an orthopedic operating room starts with a deceptively simple constraint: the surgical table must move to positions that general surgery tables do not require, and the ceiling infrastructure must not obstruct C-arm imaging at any of those positions.

**Positioning range and load capacity.** Orthopedic procedures involve patient positioning that places extreme mechanical loads on the surgical table. A 400-pound patient placed in steep lateral decubitus with full trunk and leg support requires a table that can hold the load at every angle without drift. Tables that drift under patient weight — a phenomenon called "table creep" — are a documented cause of surgical site complications in orthopedic procedures and a persistent maintenance issue with lower-quality table platforms.

**C-arm imaging integration.** Orthopedic surgery is imaging-intensive. Fluoroscopic C-arm imaging during spine, hip, knee, and shoulder procedures requires a surgical table with a radiolucent top section — not just a radiolucent leg section — so the C-arm can sweep across the operative field without obstruction. Table bases must be positioned to allow the C-arm isocenter to center on the operative site. Boom systems, light heads, and anesthesia equipment must be routed to avoid the C-arm sweep arc.

**Implant tray and equipment real estate.** Orthopedic procedures use large instrument sets — hip replacement systems, spine screw sets, fracture fixation trays — that require back table space and often a dedicated implant table. Room layout must accommodate two scrub technician positions and multiple back tables simultaneously.

**Bone work and fluid management.** Oscillating saws, drills, and reamers generate bone dust and spray. Powered irrigation systems generate large fluid volumes. Floor, ceiling, and boom drainage configurations must account for these realities in ways that general OR design does not always anticipate.

### Andrews Frame and Jackson Frame Integration

The two dominant prone positioning systems for spine surgery — the Jackson open frame and the Andrews frame — are both compatible with Skytron table platforms. Surgiquip provides positioning system consultations as part of its equipment specification services.

**Jackson open frame:** Mounted over the Skytron base, the Jackson system places the patient in prone position on a rigid frame while allowing the C-arm to sweep across the entire spine without obstruction from the table base. This is the dominant configuration for lumbar spine fusion procedures in Texas academic medical centers.

**Andrews frame:** The Andrews frame positions the patient in a kneeling-prone orientation that flexes the lumbar spine and opens the interlaminar spaces — the primary positioning advantage for posterior lumbar decompression and microdiscectomy procedures. Andrews-compatible Skytron table configurations are available for facilities with high lumbar decompression volume.

## Ceiling-Mount Boom Systems in Orthopedic ORs

Ceiling-mount equipment boom systems deliver the same core benefit in orthopedic suites that they deliver in general ORs: utilities off the floor and walls, positioned precisely and locked safely above the sterile field. In orthopedic environments, boom positioning carries additional requirements driven by C-arm imaging workflows and the physical volume of orthopedic patient positioning systems.

### C-arm Arc Clearance — The Primary Boom Design Constraint

In an orthopedic OR, the ceiling-mount boom configuration must be designed around the C-arm arc — the sweep path of the C-arm image intensifier as it rotates around the patient and table. Booms that intrude into the C-arm sweep arc require the radiologic technologist to reposition the arc for every image, which adds time to every fluoroscopic case and increases cumulative radiation exposure for everyone in the room.

Surgiquip engineers ceiling-mount boom configurations for orthopedic ORs by mapping the C-arm arc for the specific C-arm model(s) used in the facility. Pendant boom positions, boom arm lengths, and light head positions are all specified relative to the C-arm arc to ensure the room can operate at full efficiency without imaging workflow interruptions.

At **Memorial Hermann Orthopedic & Spine Hospital** in Houston — a 10-OR dedicated orthopedic facility — Surgiquip designed and installed a complete center-mount boom system configuration across all 10 operating rooms. The installation included Aurora LED surgical lights with integrated camera modules, allowing the surgical team to capture intraoperative documentation without introducing a separate camera system that would compete with boom arm positions in the C-arm arc zone.

At **North Cypress Medical Center** (Cypress, TX, 6 OR), the boom configuration included SkyVision Integration, which connects boom-mounted monitors to the facility's OR integration system — allowing orthopedic teams to display intraoperative fluoroscopy, navigation imagery, and scope views on a single integrated display without floor-based video carts.

### Anesthesia Boom Configuration for Orthopedic Cases

Orthopedic and spine surgery often uses regional anesthesia techniques — nerve blocks, epidurals, spinal anesthetics — in addition to or instead of general anesthesia. Anesthesia boom positioning for orthopedic ORs must account for the fact that regional technique setup occurs at the patient head position, which may be lateral or prone rather than supine.

The anesthesia boom configuration Surgiquip uses in orthopedic suites positions the anesthesia pendant on a dedicated boom arm with 270-degree rotation range, allowing the anesthesia provider to swing utility connections from the standard head position to the lateral or prone head position without re-routing ventilator circuits or IV lines across the sterile field.

### Nurse Documentation Centers in Orthopedic ORs

High-volume orthopedic facilities require circulating nurse documentation workflows that keep the nurse in the room and near the sterile field — not at a remote workstation outside the OR. At Memorial Hermann Orthopedic & Spine Hospital, Surgiquip installed nurse documentation centers integrated with the boom system configuration across all 10 ORs. These nurse documentation stations provide:

- **Monitor mount at ergonomic working height** — adjustable height range for standing or seated documentation
- **Keyboard tray and input device storage** — clean storage for keyboard and mouse between documentation events, preventing accumulation on horizontal surfaces near the sterile field
- **Boom-connected routing** — power, data, and display connections routed through the boom arm without floor cabling

## Aurora LED Surgical Lighting in Orthopedic Environments

Aurora LED surgical lights are the standard Skytron lighting platform Surgiquip installs across Texas hospitals, including all 10 ORs at Memorial Hermann Orthopedic & Spine Hospital. Aurora LED's orthopedic-relevant performance characteristics:

### Deep Surgical Field Illumination

Total hip replacement, spine fusion, and total knee replacement all involve surgical fields that are deep relative to the skin surface — the surgeon's hands and instruments are inside a body cavity, and the critical anatomy is at depth. Aurora LED's optical system maintains consistent illumination intensity at depth, rather than producing a bright surface ring with a dim center that characterizes older surgical light designs.

The Aurora LED headlight configuration — a multi-source LED array rather than a single-point halogen — eliminates the central shadow that single-source lights cast when the surgeon's head or hands occlude the light source. In deep orthopedic fields, where the surgeon is frequently working with both hands inside the wound, shadow elimination directly affects surgical visualization quality.

### In-Camera Integration for Intraoperative Documentation

At Memorial Hermann Orthopedic & Spine Hospital, Surgiquip installed Aurora LED lights with integrated camera modules in the light head. The integrated camera captures the surgical field video from the light head position — directly above the wound — without requiring a separate articulating camera arm that would compete for ceiling space with the boom system.

Intraoperative documentation has become standard practice in orthopedic surgery for quality improvement, resident education, and implant registry compliance. Facilities that install integrated camera systems during OR construction or renovation avoid the cost and workflow disruption of retrofitting documentation systems into operational ORs.

### Color Rendering and Tissue Discrimination

Orthopedic surgery requires reliable color discrimination between tissue types — bone cortex, cancellous bone, ligament, tendon, neural tissue, blood vessels. Aurora LED's color rendering index (CRI > 95) provides accurate tissue color reproduction across the full surgical spectrum, supporting reliable visual differentiation during dissection and implant placement.

The Aurora LED color temperature (4,000–4,500K) is optimized for surgical tissue color rendering, avoiding the blue-shifted appearance of lower-quality LED systems that can make tissue identification more difficult.

## Planning an Orthopedic OR Suite in Texas

For hospital facilities directors, construction managers, and OR planning teams building new orthopedic surgery capacity in Texas, the planning process involves several decisions where early vendor engagement produces better outcomes.

### Table Fleet Standardization vs. Specialty Configuration

The most common planning question in orthopedic OR suite design is whether to standardize on a single table platform or to configure specific rooms for specific subspecialties — one room equipped for prone spine positioning, another for total joint, another for arthroscopy and shoulder cases.

In practice, high-volume orthopedic programs typically migrate toward a standardized fleet with a common base platform (6700B or 6701) and a modular accessory library that allows each room to be configured for any procedure. This approach maximizes scheduling flexibility, reduces staff training complexity, and simplifies preventive maintenance scheduling.

For facilities starting a new orthopedic program or building new orthopedic capacity, Surgiquip provides table fleet planning consultation — reviewing case mix projections, evaluating positioning system requirements, and recommending fleet configurations that balance initial capital cost against long-term operational flexibility.

### Boom Configuration and Room Layout Coordination

Boom systems for orthopedic ORs must be coordinated with the room layout during architectural design — not retrofitted after construction. The ceiling plenum structure must accommodate the boom mounting plate position, which is determined by the boom configuration design. Pendant arm lengths must be specified relative to the room column grid to ensure adequate reach to all table positions.

Surgiquip provides rough-in coordination drawings for ceiling-mount boom systems early in the design process, working directly with the project architect and structural engineer. Coordinating the boom mounting structure during construction eliminates the retrofit costs — typically $15,000–$40,000 per room — associated with adding structural support in completed ceiling plenums.

### Service Infrastructure Planning

Orthopedic ORs operate high-capital equipment — surgical tables at $80,000–$140,000 per unit, boom systems at $40,000–$120,000 per room, lighting systems at $25,000–$60,000 per room — that must remain operational for 15–25 year lifecycles. Preventive maintenance contracts, certified service technicians, and factory-direct parts access are not optional costs; they are the cost of protecting the capital investment and maintaining OR uptime.

Surgiquip provides preventive maintenance contracts for all installed Skytron equipment, executed by factory-certified technicians based in Houston. For major Texas health systems — Memorial Hermann, Methodist, UTMB — Surgiquip has maintained ongoing service relationships that span the full lifecycle of multiple installation generations, providing continuity of institutional knowledge about each facility's specific equipment configuration.

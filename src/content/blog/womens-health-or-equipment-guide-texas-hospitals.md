The same facility that runs laparoscopic hysterectomies in the morning may transition to robot-assisted myomectomy, followed by open gynecologic oncology in the afternoon. Each procedure imposes different mechanical, lighting, and boom system requirements — and the equipment infrastructure must accommodate all of them without compromise.

## GYN Surgical ORs Are Not General ORs with a Different Patient

Facilities planners who approach GYN OR equipment procurement as a standard OR build — same table, same boom layout, same lighting — consistently encounter operational problems within the first year after opening. GYN surgical suites have specific equipment demands that a generic OR configuration does not meet:

**Extreme Trendelenburg positioning requirements.** Laparoscopic and robotic gynecologic procedures require steep Trendelenburg positioning — typically 25° to 45° of head-down tilt — to displace bowel from the pelvis and create visualization for the endoscopic camera. Standard OR tables max out at 20° to 25° Trendelenburg. A table specified for a GYN OR must achieve and hold 30°–45° reliably, with lateral tilt capability for combined access procedures, and must support that positioning under patient weights that increasingly exceed 250 lbs in Texas facilities serving bariatric-comorbid populations.

**Camera-primary lighting.** Open abdominal surgery requires high-output overhead lighting. Laparoscopic GYN surgery requires the opposite: the room must be dim enough for the surgeon to read the laparoscopic monitor clearly, while the overhead light must provide adequate ambient illumination for circulating nurses and anesthesia providers who are not looking at the monitor. Lights that cannot dim smoothly to near-off while maintaining position and control create a genuine workflow problem in robotic and laparoscopic GYN ORs.

**Robotic arm clearance as a primary constraint.** A da Vinci Surgical System deployed in a GYN OR has a physical footprint that constrains every other piece of ceiling-mount equipment in the room. The robot is docked at the patient's perineum in steep Trendelenburg. Its arms extend across the patient's lower abdomen. Boom systems, monitor arms, and lighting positions must be planned around the robot's swept volume — not retrofitted after the robot is installed.

**Minimally invasive instrument processing volume.** GYN laparoscopic cases generate laparoscopes, trocars, and robotic instrument sets that require careful sterile processing. SPD teams at high-volume GYN programs process these sets at a rate that places meaningful load on instrument washers, sterilizers, and tracking systems. The SPD infrastructure that supports a GYN program must be sized for the volume — not just the case count on paper.

### Specialty Access Positioning

Several GYN procedures require access geometry that standard OR tables do not natively support:

**Lithotomy position with steep Trendelenburg.** Combined lithotomy-Trendelenburg is the standard robotic hysterectomy position. The table must extend the perineum past the table edge for robotic port placement, maintain steep Trendelenburg under load, and support leg holders that are compatible with the robot's docking geometry. **Perineal access for combined GYN-colorectal cases.** A subset of GYN oncology procedures involve simultaneous access to the abdominal cavity and perineum. ## Lighting for Laparoscopic and Robotic GYN

Surgical lighting in a GYN OR must serve two incompatible requirements simultaneously: provide high-output illumination for open cases and wound inspection, and dim to near-zero without losing position control for laparoscopic and robotic cases where monitor visualization is primary.

### Aurora LED: The Camera-Compatible Light

The Skytron Aurora LED surgical light system — the light platform Surgiquip has installed across the most comprehensive Southeast Texas hospital build-outs — is designed explicitly for this dual requirement.

**Camera-grade color rendering.** Aurora's LED spectrum achieves CRI 97 and R9 95 — meaning tissue color under Aurora LED is rendered at a fidelity that allows accurate color discrimination for margin assessment, tissue type identification, and abnormal tissue recognition. For robotic GYN oncology cases where the surgeon is reading tissue color through a camera at 10× magnification, lighting spectrum is a genuine clinical variable.

**Smooth dimming to near-zero.** Aurora LED dims continuously from full output (≥160,000 lux) to minimal ambient illumination without banding or flicker. During laparoscopic and robotic GYN cases, the overhead light dims to ambient while the in-room illumination remains adequate for circulating nurse, anesthesia, and scrub tech tasks. The light holds its last position through the dimming cycle, and re-illuminates to the same position and output when open inspection is needed.

**No infrared heat.** High-output conventional surgical lights generate significant IR heat at the operative field — a factor in prolonged robotic GYN cases where tissue desiccation affects visualization and margin quality. Aurora LED's solid-state emitters produce output without the IR component of incandescent or halogen sources. For a 4-hour robotic hysterectomy, the difference in operative field temperature is clinically relevant.

**In-light camera system.** Aurora's in-light camera mount positions the documentation camera inside the light head, recording from the same optical axis as the surgeon's line of sight. For GYN oncology programs documenting surgical margins, pathology exposure, and lymph node dissection sequences, in-light camera recording is a clinical workflow tool — not an optional add-on.

## Boom System Design for Robotic GYN ORs

The ceiling-mount boom system in a robotic GYN OR is the most geometrically constrained element of the room. The da Vinci robot — docked at the perineal end of the table in steep Trendelenburg — occupies the lower third of the room's equipment envelope. Boom systems must be designed to leave the robot's working volume unobstructed while delivering gas, electrical, and data services to anesthesia and nursing positions that are displaced by the robot's footprint.

### The Robot Footprint Problem

A da Vinci Xi system in gynecologic configuration has a reach envelope of approximately 6 feet around the docking point. Its four arms extend across the patient in the abdominal quadrant. The anesthesia team works at the patient's head — typically elevated because the table is in steep Trendelenburg — in a position that is further from the table than in standard OR configuration.

Boom systems that are positioned for standard OR use without accounting for the robot may interfere with robot arm sweep, block anesthesia access, or create clearance problems during undocking. Surgiquip's boom installation process for facilities planning robotic GYN programs includes pre-installation coordination on robot docking geometry — identifying sweep radius, arm positions in GYN configuration, and undocking clearance — before ceiling anchoring.

### Standard Robotic GYN Boom Configuration

A well-designed robotic GYN OR boom layout typically includes:

**Anesthesia boom:** Positioned at the patient's head end, angled to provide gas supply (O₂, air, N₂O, vacuum) and electrical drops to the anesthesia machine. In steep Trendelenburg, the anesthesia position relative to the patient's airway is modified — the boom must reach the working height without blocking robot undocking paths.

**Equipment/service boom:** Positioned on the surgeon-side of the room (opposite anesthesia), carrying electrical supply and data connections for the robotic console support equipment, video tower, insufflation system, and energy platform. These services cannot run across the floor in a robotic OR — they terminate at overhead positions.

**Surgeon monitor arm:** A dedicated monitor arm carrying the secondary 32"–55" display positioned for the circulating nurse and scrub tech. The robotic console has its own display system; the room monitor serves non-console team members who need visual access to the laparoscopic view.

**Documentation/data drop:** An overhead service drop positioned for the perioperative nursing workstation, keeping the documentation terminal off the floor and accessible without crossing the robot's working volume.

## Scope Reprocessing for GYN Minimally Invasive Surgery

High-volume GYN laparoscopic programs generate instrument sets that impose specific SPD demands. Laparoscopes, hysteroscopes, trocars, robotic EndoWrist instruments, and energy sealing devices all require processing pathways appropriate to their reuse classification and manufacturer requirements.

**Flexible hysteroscopes** — used for in-office and OR hysteroscopic procedures — are classified as semi-critical devices under Spaulding classification and require high-level disinfection at minimum (AER processing or manual high-level disinfectant soak). Programs with high hysteroscopy volume require sufficient AER capacity to turn hysteroscopes without delaying the OR schedule.

**Robotic instrument sets** — da Vinci EndoWrist instruments have defined reuse limits (tracking required) and require specific reprocessing per Intuitive Surgical IFU. SPD programs processing robotic GYN cases must have tracking systems, compatible washer-disinfectors with validated instrument trays, and steam sterilizer capacity to handle the instrument set cycle times without creating case delay.

## Planning a Women's Health OR Suite: Where to Start

For facilities directors and women's health program leadership planning a new GYN OR suite or renovating existing space, the planning sequence that minimizes downstream equipment conflicts follows this order:

**1. Define the robotic surgery program scope first.** If the facility is committing to da Vinci installation, the robot's docking geometry defines the OR footprint, the boom system position, and the equipment service routing before anything else is determined. Robot-first planning prevents boom installation that must be modified after robot delivery.

**2. Specify table Trendelenburg range against the procedure profile.** A facility doing robotic hysterectomy needs 45° Trendelenburg capacity. A facility doing only laparoscopic tubal ligation needs 25°. Overspecifying costs money; underspecifying creates workarounds that affect positioning and, ultimately, case safety.

**3. Size SPD for the actual instrument count.** GYN laparoscopic programs frequently surprise SPD managers with instrument processing volume. A facility doing 12 robotic GYN cases per day is processing 12+ da Vinci sets alongside laparoscopes, energy instruments, and hysteroscopes. Sterilizer capacity and cycle time must be planned against this volume.

**4. Coordinate boom position with robot, imaging, and video tower before anchoring.** The cost of moving ceiling anchors after installation is high — both direct cost and OR downtime. Pre-installation coordination with the OR design team, the robot vendor, and the imaging equipment vendor on anchor positions and ceiling utility routing prevents this outcome.

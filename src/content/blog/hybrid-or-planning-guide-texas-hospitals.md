A hybrid operating room is no longer an exclusive feature of large academic medical centers. As minimally invasive and image-guided surgical techniques expand across cardiovascular, orthopedic, neurosurgical, and oncologic specialties, Texas community hospitals and regional health systems are increasingly evaluating hybrid OR builds — either as new construction or as conversions of existing surgical suites.

The decision involves far more than imaging equipment selection. A hybrid OR is a multi-system integration project that touches room architecture, structural engineering, HVAC, electrical, medical gas, equipment infrastructure, and surgical workflow simultaneously. This guide outlines what Texas facilities teams, OR directors, and capital planning committees need to know before breaking ground.

## What Defines a Hybrid Operating Room

A hybrid OR combines full surgical capability with integrated advanced imaging in a single, purpose-built space. Unlike a standard OR (where a portable C-arm might be wheeled in for fluoroscopy), a hybrid OR integrates fixed imaging directly into the room infrastructure — typically a ceiling-mounted flat-panel detector system for biplane or monoplane fluoroscopy, CT integration, or robotic-arm-mounted imaging.

The functional result: surgeons can perform complex open, endovascular, or minimally invasive procedures while simultaneously using real-time high-resolution imaging — without moving the patient, breaking sterile field, or waiting for portable equipment to be positioned.

**Primary specialties driving hybrid OR demand in Texas hospitals:**

- **Cardiovascular and vascular surgery** — TAVR (transcatheter aortic valve replacement), EVAR (endovascular aortic repair), hybrid coronary revascularization
- **Neurosurgery and spine** — intraoperative CT/fluoroscopy for tumor resection, spinal navigation, cerebrovascular procedures
- **Orthopedics** — trauma fixation, complex joint reconstruction with intraoperative verification, pediatric orthopedic procedures
- **Oncologic surgery** — tumor ablation with imaging confirmation, sentinel node biopsy with radioisotope guidance
- **Cardiac electrophysiology** — catheter ablation, device implantation with fluoroscopic guidance

## Room Size and Structural Requirements

Hybrid ORs are substantially larger than standard surgical suites — and that size requirement is non-negotiable. Standard ORs in Texas acute care facilities typically run 400–600 sq ft. Hybrid ORs typically require:

- **800–1,000+ sq ft of floor space** for the combined imaging/surgical footprint
- **Ceiling height of 11–13 feet minimum** to accommodate ceiling-mounted imaging arms, surgical light booms, and equipment pendant systems
- **Floor-level gantry or ceiling track infrastructure** for the imaging system — structural reinforcement of the ceiling is almost always required, often requiring a dedicated steel frame above the ceiling
- **Control room or equipment alcove** (separate or adjacent) for imaging workstations, recording systems, and cardiac monitoring — typically 120–200 sq ft

If you are converting an existing OR rather than building new, a structural engineering assessment of the ceiling load capacity is the first prerequisite. Retrofit hybrid OR projects frequently uncover that the existing ceiling structure cannot support the imaging equipment load without significant reinforcement — a finding that should be scoped and priced before committing to the project timeline.

## Electrical and Medical Gas Infrastructure

Hybrid ORs are among the most electrically intensive spaces in a hospital. Planning must account for:

**Electrical:**
- Isolated power panels — required per NFPA 99 for wet procedure locations; hybrid ORs nearly always qualify
- Dedicated circuits for imaging equipment (400–600A service in some configurations), surgical lights, and equipment booms
- UPS (uninterruptible power supply) considerations for imaging systems and critical monitoring
- Low-voltage infrastructure: fiber optic runs, video distribution cabling, OR integration system wiring

**Medical Gas:**
- Additional outlet zones to serve both the surgical team and anesthesia positions on multiple sides of the table
- NFPA 99 Chapter 5 zone valve placement — the imaging arm's travel envelope must not conflict with zone valve locations
- Vacuum and WAGD (Waste Anesthetic Gas Disposal) outlets positioned for anesthesia access from multiple approach angles
- Consider additional nitrogen outlets if pneumatic surgical tools are used

Engage your AHJ (Authority Having Jurisdiction) early. In Texas, this typically involves the Texas Department of State Health Services Health Care Facilities division, your accreditation body (Joint Commission or DNV GL), and your local fire marshal. Design review and plan approval timelines vary by jurisdiction and should be factored into the project schedule.

## HVAC and Radiation Shielding

**HVAC requirements** for a hybrid OR follow ASHRAE 170-2021 (Ventilation of Health Care Facilities) standards for Class B ORs:
- Minimum 20 air changes per hour (ACH), with at least 4 ACH outside air
- Positive pressure relative to adjacent spaces
- Temperature control: 68–75°F with 60–65% relative humidity
- ULPA or HEPA filtration

The imaging equipment itself generates significant heat. The imaging system vendor's specifications will define heat load requirements — this must be integrated into the mechanical design early, as oversized HVAC systems that weren't planned for in the original design are expensive corrections.

**Radiation shielding** is a code requirement, not an option. Texas hospitals follow Texas Department of State Health Services radiation control regulations (25 TAC Chapter 289). A medical physicist must perform a shielding calculation based on:
- The specific imaging equipment's workload and use factors
- Room geometry and occupancy of adjacent spaces
- Whether the adjacent spaces are controlled or uncontrolled areas

Shielding is typically integrated into walls and sometimes the floor and ceiling. Do not finalize room design without a completed shielding calculation — retrofitting shielding after construction is disproportionately expensive.

## Equipment Integration: Surgical Lights, Booms, and Tables

The imaging system (from Siemens, Philips, GE, or a similar vendor) is only one component of a hybrid OR. The room's surgical infrastructure must be designed to work with it — and this is where OR equipment vendors with hybrid OR experience become critical.

### Surgical Lights with X-Ray Compatibility

Standard OR lights are not hybrid OR lights. In a hybrid OR, the ceiling space is shared between the imaging arm, surgical light booms, and equipment pendant systems. Surgical lights must be:

- **X-ray compatible** — components must not create artifacts in fluoroscopic imaging when positioned near the imaging field
- **Designed for ceiling-integrated mounting** that does not conflict with the imaging system's swing arc
- **Capable of repositioning to the head-end of the table** when imaging is in use over the surgical field

Skytron's surgical lighting systems — including the Stellar LED and Eclipse LED product families — are designed with hybrid OR integration in mind. Mounting configurations, arm reach, and the light head's X-ray compatibility are engineered to coexist with ceiling-mounted imaging systems rather than compete with them for overhead real estate.

### Ceiling Boom Systems

Equipment booms in a hybrid OR must be positioned to serve the surgical team without entering the imaging system's sweep path. Key considerations:

- **Boom positioning relative to imaging arc** — the imaging system vendor provides a sweep zone diagram that must be respected in boom placement
- **Service capacity** — hybrid ORs require booms with higher electrical and gas outlet density to serve anesthesia, imaging-side nursing, and the surgical team simultaneously
- **Reach and rotation range** — longer boom arms are often required because the imaging system occupies overhead space that would otherwise be available for shorter boom configurations
- **Service runs** — power, gas, data, and fiber runs to boom mounting points must be roughed in during construction; retrofitting is expensive

Skytron's boom systems — configurable for power/data/gas combinations, multiple mounting styles, and custom arm lengths — are a common specification for hybrid OR projects where the installer has authorization to provide a fully integrated Skytron room.

### Surgical Table Selection

The surgical table in a hybrid OR must be radiolucent across the full working length to allow unobstructed fluoroscopic imaging in any position. Requirements:

- **Full-length carbon fiber tabletop** — partial carbon fiber tops (radiolucent in the torso section only) are insufficient for most hybrid OR procedures
- **High weight capacity at Trendelenburg and reverse-Trendelenburg** — hybrid cases often involve complex patient positioning; table capacity ratings at position are more relevant than flat-position ratings
- **Motorized height and positioning** — hand-crank positioning is a workflow liability in a room where the imaging arm may need to be cleared and repositioned between table position changes
- **Table/imaging system compatibility** — some imaging systems have specific table centerline, height range, and end-clearance requirements; verify compatibility before specifying the table

Skytron's 6000 series surgical tables are a common pairing in hybrid OR configurations, with full-length carbon fiber top options, high positioning flexibility, and compatibility with major ceiling-mounted imaging platforms.

## Workflow and Staffing Considerations

Technology integration without workflow integration produces a room that is underutilized. Before finalizing the hybrid OR design, the facilities and OR leadership team should address:

- **Who operates the imaging system?** — in cardiovascular hybrid ORs, a dedicated imaging tech or cath lab RT is typical; in general surgery or spine applications, OR staff often receive cross-training
- **How is the patient moved between surgical and imaging positions?** — the table travel range and imaging system positioning must support this transition without additional patient transfers
- **How does the OR team communicate during imaging?** — lead aprons, ceiling-mounted radiation monitors, and control room sight lines must be designed for the actual workflow
- **How is the room cleaned and turned between cases?** — more equipment in a larger room means longer turnover times; OR efficiency models should be recalibrated before launch

## Vendor Selection: Separating Authorized Dealers from Brokers

A hybrid OR project involves multiple vendors, but the OR equipment provider — surgical lights, tables, and booms — should be an authorized dealer with documented hybrid OR experience.

**Five questions to ask any OR equipment vendor for a hybrid OR project:**

1. **Are you an authorized dealer for every product you're specifying?** — OEM authorization matters for warranty coverage, service response, and parts availability. A broker who transacts equipment but is not factory-authorized provides no coverage once the project closes.

2. **Have your technicians installed your boom and light systems in hybrid OR configurations before?** — Hybrid OR installations have specific requirements around sweep-zone compliance, ceiling reinforcement interfaces, and imaging system coordination. Generic OR installation experience is not equivalent.

3. **Can you provide references from hybrid OR projects in Texas hospitals?** — Texas facilities operate under TDSHS regulations and Joint Commission or DNV GL standards. Familiarity with the Texas regulatory environment is not universal.

4. **What is your post-installation service model?** — The hybrid OR is not a build-and-hand-off project. Ongoing PM, emergency service response, and parts supply for the surgical equipment are part of the lifecycle cost.

5. **How do you coordinate with the imaging system vendor on ceiling interface requirements?** — The OR equipment installer and the imaging system vendor must coordinate on ceiling rough-in requirements, mounting point locations, and sweep-zone compliance. Ask how that coordination is managed on projects you can verify.

## Project Timeline Benchmarks for Texas Hospitals

Hybrid OR projects in Texas acute care facilities typically require:

| Phase | Duration |
|-------|----------|
| Feasibility / needs assessment | 1–3 months |
| Architect/engineer design development | 3–6 months |
| State regulatory review (TDSHS plan review) | 4–8 weeks (varies by facility classification) |
| Construction / renovation | 6–18 months depending on scope |
| Equipment procurement and lead times | 16–26 weeks for imaging systems; 8–16 weeks for OR equipment |
| Installation, commissioning, and training | 4–8 weeks |

Equipment lead times are the most common schedule risk. Imaging system and surgical equipment lead times have extended significantly since 2021. Beginning the equipment procurement process early — with letters of intent and procurement orders placed during design development — is standard practice for projects seeking to minimize total timeline.

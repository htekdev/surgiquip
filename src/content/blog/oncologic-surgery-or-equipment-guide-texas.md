## What Makes an Oncology OR Different

Oncology OR planning starts with a simple reality: cancer surgery is often a **long-case environment**. Eight-hour, ten-hour, and even twelve-plus-hour cases are not outliers in hepatobiliary, gynecologic, orthopedic oncology, retroperitoneal, thoracic, and spine tumor service lines. That duration changes what "good enough" equipment means. A light that drifts in color temperature after several hours, a table that loses stability at steep positioning angles, or a boom layout that is manageable for a one-team case but chaotic for a five-person field will all become operational liabilities in oncology.

The second major difference is **multidisciplinary staffing density**. A cancer case may involve the primary resection team, reconstructive support, anesthesia, surgical assistants, perfusion or specialty support in selected procedures, pathology coordination, and imaging staff. The room must be organized for multiple active users at once, not just a single surgical approach. This directly affects boom parking positions, monitor placement, cable management, and how much usable floor space remains after mobile equipment enters the room.

Positioning demands are also more complex than in many general ORs. A cancer center room may need to support:

- **Lithotomy** for gynecologic and colorectal oncology
- **Steep Trendelenburg** for robotic-assisted gynecologic cancer cases
- **Prone positioning** for spinal tumor or posterior sarcoma access
- Frequent accessory changes for extended open exposures or reconstruction

These are not edge-case accessories bolted onto a commodity table. They are primary workflow requirements. If the table platform, rail system, and accessory ecosystem are not chosen with oncology in mind, staff end up building awkward workarounds that compromise efficiency and ergonomics.

Oncology suites also place greater emphasis on **intraoperative imaging**. Guided biopsies, fluoroscopy, intraoperative CT, O-arm navigation, sentinel node mapping workflows, and margin-marking processes all depend on equipment that preserves imaging access. Carbon fiber surfaces, C-arm arc clearance, navigation camera sight lines, and boom placement around imaging envelopes all become design variables that should be coordinated before procurement.

Finally, infection control standards take on heightened significance because many oncology patients are **immunocompromised**. That affects not just sterile processing, but also the physical design of the room infrastructure. Smooth wipe-down surfaces, sealed lighting housings, cleanable boom arms, reliable preventive maintenance, and documented compliance processes matter more when the patients in the room are clinically less tolerant of contamination risk.

## Lighting for Tumor Margin Assessment

Lighting in oncology is not just about bright illumination. It is fundamentally about **visual discrimination**. Surgeons evaluating tissue planes and suspected tumor boundaries need lighting that preserves subtle color differences over the entire length of a case. For that reason, facilities teams should evaluate cancer-suite lighting against stricter color rendering criteria than they might use in a basic general OR refresh.

The specification threshold that deserves attention is **CRI ≥ 97 with R9 ≥ 95**. CRI measures overall color accuracy, while R9 specifically reflects strong red rendering performance. In tissue-based surgery, that red fidelity matters: perfusion, vascularity, inflammation, necrotic change, and subtle differences in soft tissue color all become easier to interpret under high-R9 lighting. When a light source performs poorly in the red spectrum, tissue margin discrimination becomes harder, not easier.

Long-case behavior matters just as much as the specification sheet. Incandescent halogens and many early-generation LEDs can shift in color temperature or degrade in effective output over time. In an oncology room, where exposure may remain open for most of a workday, the better question is not just what the light delivers in minute one, but what it still delivers in hour eight or hour twelve. **Aurora LED** is relevant in this context because it maintains stable color temperature and consistent field quality across extended case durations rather than warming the field or drifting as the procedure progresses.

Another meaningful oncology benefit is that Aurora emits **no IR heat radiation into the surgical field**. Over long cases, excess radiant heat contributes to surgeon discomfort, field drying, and overall room fatigue. Eliminating infrared heat load does not replace environmental controls, but it does improve the working environment in cases where a fixed exposure may remain open for hours.

Facilities evaluating documentation workflows should also consider the role of the **Aurora in-light camera**. In oncology, intraoperative photo capture can support tumor documentation, pathology communication, teaching, and medical record completeness. Integrating documentation into the light head keeps that capability aligned with the operative field rather than relying on improvised photography or secondary devices.

## Surgical Tables for Oncologic Procedures

The surgical table is the mechanical platform that determines whether an oncology room can truly adapt across service lines. For many Texas hospitals, the right strategy is not choosing a table that is merely versatile on paper, but one that can safely support the most demanding oncologic positions without compromising imaging access or stability.

For **gynecologic and colorectal oncology**, the table needs a robust **lithotomy adapter system** with accessories that can be positioned repeatably and securely for extended procedures. These are not short, low-complexity cases. Padding, leg support geometry, rail compatibility, and stability over many hours all matter because staff cannot afford gradual drift in positioning once a case is deep into a pelvic dissection.

For robotic-assisted gynecologic oncology, **steep Trendelenburg** is a core requirement. Many rooms are nominally capable of Trendelenburg, but oncology teams should specifically verify performance at **30 degrees or greater**, under real patient loads, with the accessory configuration in place. The relevant issue is not just whether the table reaches the angle, but whether it does so while preserving patient stability, maintaining lock integrity, and avoiding flex or micro-movement during long robotic cases.

For **spine tumor** and selected **sarcoma** procedures, the room must support safe **prone positioning** and the accessory ecosystem that comes with it. That includes compatibility with prone frames, imaging access for spinal instrumentation or tumor localization, and sufficient load performance when the table is configured far from neutral.

Imaging compatibility is a defining oncology requirement. Facilities planning for intraoperative CT, fluoroscopy, or advanced navigation should strongly favor **carbon fiber tops** or imaging-optimized configurations that minimize artifact. If the cancer service line depends on guided biopsies, margin marking under fluoroscopy, or intraoperative CT confirmation, partial radiolucency is often not enough. The table should be selected as part of the imaging workflow, not added after it.

Weight capacity and **multi-hour stability** also deserve more scrutiny in oncology than in routine procurement exercises. Tables should be evaluated not only in flat position, but at their clinically relevant extremes: steep Trendelenburg, extended accessory load, prone setup, or imaging-ready configuration. The room's positioning safety margin is only as good as the table's least stable operative position.

## Boom System Design for Oncology

Boom planning in an oncology room is often where otherwise strong capital projects succeed or fail operationally. A cancer suite may have enough square footage on paper, yet still feel crowded and inefficient if the boom arms are not configured around the way multidisciplinary oncology cases actually unfold.

At minimum, many oncologic cases require a **five-plus-person sterile field ecosystem** once you account for the resection team, anesthesia, scrub support, assistants, and specialty equipment. Add reconstructive participation, neuromonitoring hardware, or advanced imaging carts, and the demand on room organization increases quickly. The goal of boom design is not merely to get outlets off the wall. It is to create predictable parking positions that preserve access for each team without forcing repeated room reconfiguration during a case.

In practical terms, oncology boom layouts should define where equipment parks during:

- **Initial oncologic resection**
- **Reconstructive handoff or shared field work**
- **Anesthesia head-of-table access**
- **Scrub support and instrument circulation**

When those parking positions are not modeled in advance, boom arms drift into ad hoc locations, monitors end up in sight-line conflicts, and the floor space advantage of a ceiling-mounted system is partially lost.

Cable management is another overlooked oncology requirement. In spinal, retroperitoneal, and select thoracic tumor cases, **IONM cable routing** can become a serious clutter source if the room was not planned around it. Ceiling-delivered power and data help, but the boom configuration must still preserve clear routing paths that do not interfere with sterile movement or imaging access.

For minimally invasive oncology, the room also needs to support the **laparoscopic or robotic camera stack**, associated monitors, and any retractor-arm integration without overwhelming one side of the table. Monitor booms, equipment carriers, and data/power access should be balanced so the visualization platform is close enough to the operative team while still clearing the travel paths of imaging devices or additional staff.

**Retractor arm integration** is especially relevant for open abdominal, thoracic, and sarcoma work. These accessory systems create additional forces and positional constraints around the table. If booms, monitor arms, and accessory rails are all competing for the same physical zone, the case setup becomes slower and less reproducible.

## Intraoperative Imaging and Navigation Integration

Cancer surgery increasingly overlaps with imaging and navigation platforms that were once considered specialty-only tools. As a result, oncology room design should assume that intraoperative imaging is a primary room requirement, not a rare exception.

One of the most useful planning concepts is the **180-degree C-arm arc rule**. For guided biopsies, tumor margin marking, and sentinel node mapping workflows, the room should be laid out so a mobile C-arm can achieve a full working arc without repeated equipment repositioning. When booms, tables, light heads, or monitor arms intrude into that envelope, case efficiency drops and imaging quality can suffer.

Rooms that may use an **O-arm** or similar advanced imaging platform need even more dedicated spatial planning. The issue is not just floor footprint, but the three-dimensional clearance required for gantry movement, staff safety zones, and visibility of the patient and sterile field during image acquisition. A room that can technically fit an O-arm but forces everything else into compromise positions is not truly O-arm ready.

Navigation systems create their own infrastructure rules. Optical platforms such as **Stryker Nav** or **Medtronic O-arm/navigation combinations** require clear lines of sight for the camera array and reliable parking locations for related equipment. Ceiling-mounted arms and large monitors should be positioned to avoid blocking those sight lines. This is a detail that often gets noticed too late if equipment packages are procured independently rather than as a coordinated oncology-room system.

The **Aurora in-light camera** adds another integration benefit by simplifying field documentation. In cases where intraoperative photo capture is valuable for pathology communication, teaching, or case records, embedding documentation at the light head reduces the number of separate devices entering the room and helps preserve sterile workflow.

## Sterile Processing for Oncologic Instrument Sets

Sterile processing is easy to underestimate when people talk about oncology OR equipment, but cancer surgery often pushes SPD complexity to a level that general procedure planning does not. A major retroperitoneal resection, multiquadrant abdominal oncology case, or reconstructive-oncology workflow can involve **400 or more instruments** across multiple trays. The OR cannot remain high-performing if the reprocessing side of the operation is underbuilt or inconsistently maintained.

For oncology, **AAMI ST79 compliance** deserves special emphasis because the patient population is frequently immunocompromised. That does not change the standard itself, but it does raise the operational stakes of every missed maintenance interval, sterilizer inconsistency, or poorly documented process. Facilities leaders should view sterile processing not as a back-of-house commodity, but as a direct extension of cancer-suite infection prevention.

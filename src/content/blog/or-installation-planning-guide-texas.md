An operating room installation — whether new construction, renovation, or hybrid OR conversion — is one of the most complex capital projects a hospital facilities team will undertake. Equipment selection is only part of the challenge. Coordination, compliance, and installation sequencing determine whether a room opens on time and performs as designed.

This guide covers the key planning phases Texas hospitals should work through before the first ceiling anchor goes in.

## Phase 1: Equipment Selection & Scope Definition

Before any construction coordination begins, define the complete equipment scope:

**Surgical Tables**
- Procedure type drives table selection (orthopedic, cardiovascular, general surgery, hybrid)
- Skytron's table lineup, for example, ranges from general-purpose configurations to specialty tables with carbon fiber tops for intraoperative imaging

**Overhead Surgical Lighting**
- Single vs. dual-head configurations
- LED luminescence requirements (typically 160,000 lux minimum for main field)
- Satellite light positioning relative to pendant boom placement

**Ceiling-Mount Equipment Booms**
- Single-arm vs. double-arm; equipment vs. anesthesia vs. hybrid configurations
- Gas service (O₂, N₂O, medical air, vacuum), electrical, and data requirements per arm
- NFPA 99 Healthcare Facilities Code is the compliance benchmark

**Auxiliary Systems**
- AV/4K imaging integration
- OR control panels
- Video documentation systems

## Phase 2: Pre-Construction Coordination

Your equipment supplier should be at the table during construction coordination — not just at delivery. Key touchpoints:

**Structural**
- Ceiling height and slab thickness for boom anchoring
- Load capacity calculations for ceiling-mount equipment
- HVAC/laminar flow ceiling coordination (booms and light heads must clear ventilation envelope)

**Mechanical/Electrical**
- Confirm rough-in locations for gas outlets, electrical connections, and data drops **before** concrete or drywall
- Coordinate with the GC on conduit runs and junction box placements
- Electrical load calculations for surgical lighting (often 20–30A dedicated circuits per head)

**Infection Control**
- Coordinate with infection control on temporary wall placement during installation
- Define clean/dirty separation procedures for deliveries

## Phase 3: Installation Sequencing

Most OR equipment installations follow this general sequence:

1. **Structural rough-in** — boom anchor plates, ceiling inserts, conduit
2. **Mechanical rough-in** — gas outlets, electrical service, data ports
3. **Equipment delivery staging** — coordinate loading dock access, staging area
4. **Ceiling-mount equipment installation** — booms, then light heads
5. **Table installation and positioning** — after booms are commissioned
6. **AV/data integration** — connect imaging and documentation systems
7. **Gas outlet commissioning** — pressure testing per NFPA 99
8. **Electrical testing and load verification**
9. **Joint commissioning walkthrough** — facilities, infection control, perioperative team
10. **Staff training** — surgical nursing, biomed team

## Phase 4: Compliance & Documentation

Texas hospitals must maintain compliance documentation for Joint Commission, DNV, or CMS surveys. Your installation partner should provide:

- **As-built drawings** for ceiling-mount equipment
- **NFPA 99 gas outlet test records**
- **Equipment serial numbers and model documentation** for the facility asset register
- **Warranty documentation** — both equipment warranty and installation warranty
- **Manufacturer PM requirements** — for biomed planning

## Common Planning Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Ordering equipment before ceiling details are confirmed** — boom anchor locations must match the structural layout
2. **Skipping the pre-install site visit** — experienced installers catch rough-in errors before they become change orders
3. **Underestimating staff training time** — surgical staff need hands-on time with new tables and lights before go-live

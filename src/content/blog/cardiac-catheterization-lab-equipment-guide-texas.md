## What Makes a Cath Lab Different From a Standard OR

On the surface, a cardiac catheterization lab looks similar to a surgical suite: it has ceiling-mounted equipment, overhead lighting, a procedure table, and a sterile field. Underneath, the engineering requirements are substantially different.

**Fixed interventional imaging dominates the room.** A cath lab is designed around a fixed flat-panel detector system — typically ceiling-mounted, floor-mounted, or both — from vendors like Siemens (Artis series), Philips (Azurion, Allura), or GE (Discovery, Innova). The imaging system's mounting configuration, swing radius, and isocenter height determine where everything else goes: the ceiling grid, boom arm positions, table location, and service access corridors.

**Room shielding requirements are extensive.** Ionizing radiation during fluoroscopic-guided procedures requires lead-lined walls, doors, and ceiling panels. Structural reinforcement for the imaging system suspension adds significant floor and ceiling loading. Facilities teams need to engage radiation physics consultants early — before any equipment is specified.

**Procedure durations are longer.** Cardiac, vascular, and structural heart procedures in a cath lab routinely run three to eight hours. Heat generated by overhead equipment, the imaging C-arm, and multiple monitors affects both staff comfort and patient physiology. Equipment selection should account for thermal management in ways that shorter OR cases don't require.

**Table-to-imaging integration is non-negotiable.** The procedure table must be compatible with the imaging system's isocenter geometry. Radiolucent table tops, precise tabletop positioning (longitudinal, lateral, Trendelenburg, reverse Trendelenburg, tilt), and the ability to park the table precisely under the C-arm are design requirements, not optional features.

## Ceiling-Mount Boom Systems: The Central Engineering Challenge

For most Texas hospitals, the most complex element of a cath lab installation is not the imaging system itself — it's the ceiling-mount boom infrastructure that must coexist with it.

A standard surgical OR boom system provides power, gas, data, and equipment positioning for anesthesia and nurse workstations. In a cath lab, that function remains, but the geometry is complicated by the imaging system's clearance envelope.

### The Rotation Radius Problem

Modern fixed-panel cath lab systems — particularly biplane systems like the Siemens Artis icono biplane — rotate in both primary and lateral detector arcs, sometimes sweeping 180 degrees or more. Any ceiling structure within that rotation envelope must either be relocated or eliminated. This creates a zone around the table where traditional suspended equipment is not viable.

The solution is **strategic offset positioning** of boom arms: anchoring them at ceiling points that remain clear of the imaging sweep zone, then using extended cantilever arms to bring equipment positioning back over the table periphery. This requires detailed planning with the imaging vendor's installation templates before a single structural anchor is placed.

In the **Memorial Hermann Southwest** Cath Lab installation, Surgiquip engineers faced exactly this challenge: the imaging configuration required custom-length extended arms on Skytron Center Mount Boom systems to reach usable equipment positions without interfering with the C-arm's sweep radius. The result was a fully functional anesthesia and nurse documentation boom arrangement that cleared the imaging envelope — but it required engineering coordination that a standard OR boom quote would never have anticipated.

### Pendant vs. Center Mount in Cath Labs

**Pendant systems** (single ceiling drops with articulating arms) are often specified in cath labs because their smaller ceiling footprint reduces the chances of imaging conflicts. They work well for anesthesia drops at the head of the table, where imaging sweep is less likely to create conflicts.

**Center mount systems** (the predominant approach Surgiquip uses in full ORs) remain viable in cath labs when the room is large enough — typically 600+ square feet — and when ceiling grid planning is done with full knowledge of the imaging envelope. The advantage is equipment capacity and organization: a center mount system can carry more service drops, monitor arms, and documentation stations than a single pendant.

For large, high-volume cath labs with complex cases, a hybrid approach is often optimal: center mount for the dominant boom zone (clear of imaging), with supplemental pendants on the imaging-adjacent side.

## Surgical Lighting in Cardiac Cath Labs

Overhead surgical lighting in a cath lab serves a different function than in a standard OR. While procedural illumination is still required, the primary light source for interventional guidance is the fluoroscopy monitor — not the overhead fixture.

This changes the specification calculus in several ways:

**Light intensity can be lower than OR standards.** A standard surgical OR is specified at 40,000–160,000 lux. Cath lab overhead lighting is often specified at 30,000–80,000 lux — sufficient for open-arterial portions of procedures but not overdriving into fluoroscopy monitor glare.

**Heat reduction is particularly important.** Cath lab cases run long. Prolonged overhead heat output affects both sterile field conditions and staff comfort during multi-hour procedures. Modern LED systems like Skytron's Aurora LED line operate at substantially lower field temperatures than legacy halogen or early-generation LED fixtures — a meaningful benefit in a procedure environment where the room is continuously occupied for hours at a time.

**Dimming control and scene presets matter.** During fluoroscopic runs, proceduralists typically want the room dimmed to enhance monitor visibility. Lighting control systems should support quick scene switching — full procedural illumination for open portions, dimmed for fluoroscopic acquisition — ideally from a foot pedal or remote control that doesn't require the physician to break scrub.

**Positioning flexibility around the imaging envelope.** The lighting head suspension system must accommodate the same imaging clearance constraints as the boom arms. Dual-head configurations may need to be adjusted so that both heads remain accessible and positionable without entering the C-arm's rotation arc.

In the **UTMB John Sealy Hospital** installation — a major installation spanning 32 ORs, 1 Cath Lab, and 23 LDRP suites — Surgiquip coordinated lighting across a diverse set of room configurations. The Cath Lab component required careful light arm placement to maintain practical positioning range while clearing the imaging system.

## Procedure Tables for Cardiac Catheterization

Selecting a procedure table for a cath lab involves requirements that don't apply to standard surgical tables:

**Full radiolucency.** The tabletop must be fully transparent to X-ray — no metal hardware in the imaging field. Standard surgical tables with metal frames or actuator housings in the tabletop area are not appropriate for interventional imaging procedures.

**Long longitudinal travel.** To position the patient at the imaging isocenter for procedures at the head (coronary, carotid) and the abdomen/pelvis (aortic, peripheral vascular), the table must slide longitudinally under a fixed ceiling mount — or the room must accommodate a floor-mounted system that travels with the table.

**Float-top or sliding tabletop capability.** Bi-directional floating top motion allows repositioning during the procedure without moving the patient's body. For peripheral vascular and electrophysiology cases, the ability to move the imaging field to different body zones without repositioning is essential.

**Weight capacity.** Cardiac patients frequently present with comorbidities including obesity. Bariatric weight ratings — typically 500 lbs or above for a cath lab table — should be specified for any general-purpose cath lab that will see a cross-section of the cardiovascular patient population.

## NFPA 99 and Preventive Maintenance in Cath Labs

Cardiac catheterization labs are classified as Category 1 spaces under NFPA 99 (Health Care Facilities Code) — the highest criticality classification, applicable wherever failure of equipment support systems may cause major injury to or death of patients or caregivers.

In practical terms, this means:

- **Emergency power**: All critical systems (imaging, lighting, monitoring, gas supply) must be backed by a Type 1 Essential Electrical System — with automatic transfer in under 10 seconds.
- **Medical gas standards**: Oxygen, nitrous oxide (in applicable rooms), and medical air outlets must meet Category 1 specifications.
- **Equipment inspection frequency**: Boom systems, light heads, and table motion systems in Category 1 spaces should be inspected on a formal schedule — not just when something fails.

For Surgiquip clients across Southeast Texas, preventive maintenance agreements for cath lab equipment cover:

- Boom arm articulation and brake function testing
- Light head illuminance verification and beam uniformity testing
- Surgical table function testing across all axis of motion (longitudinal, lateral, Trendelenburg, tilt, break)
- Hydraulic system inspection (for tables with hydraulic actuators)
- Electrical connection integrity and ground continuity
- Software/firmware currency where applicable

Because equipment failures in a Category 1 space can interrupt in-progress cardiac or vascular procedures, scheduled PM cycles are a risk management tool, not just an administrative obligation.

## Why Factory Certification Matters in Cath Lab Service

Equipment in a cardiac catheterization lab operates under conditions that generic service providers cannot adequately support:

**Access to OEM firmware and calibration tools.** Modern surgical boom systems, light heads, and imaging-integrated tables involve electronic control systems that require manufacturer-specific diagnostic tools for proper service. Factory-trained technicians have access to these tools; third-party providers often do not.

**Knowledge of configuration-specific issues.** Extended-arm boom configurations, custom ceiling mount geometries, and integrated control systems have configuration-specific failure modes that aren't documented in standard service manuals. Factory training includes exposure to these edge cases.

**Parts availability.** Authorized service providers have direct access to OEM replacement parts with documented provenance — critical in a regulatory environment where parts traceability may be required for Joint Commission or FDA compliance.

**Liability and documentation.** Service records from an authorized provider carry more regulatory weight than those from an uncertified shop. When a Joint Commission surveyor reviews the maintenance history of a Category 1 OR boom system, the credentials of the service organization matter.

## Planning Your Cath Lab Equipment Investment

If your hospital or health system in Southeast Texas is planning a new cath lab or renovating an existing one, the equipment procurement sequence that produces the best outcomes typically follows this path:

1. **Finalize imaging system selection first.** The C-arm vendor's installation template drives every other equipment decision.
2. **Engage your OR equipment supplier early** — before structural design is finalized. Extended lead time for custom boom arm engineering can be six to eight weeks; discovering the need for custom arms during construction is expensive.
3. **Coordinate ceiling grid and structural reinforcement** between the imaging vendor, OR equipment supplier, and structural engineer simultaneously — not sequentially.
4. **Specify PM coverage in the equipment contract**, not as an afterthought 12 months post-installation.
5. **Validate table compatibility** with the specific imaging isocenter geometry before purchase.
